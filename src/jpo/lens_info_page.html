Why Lens Thickness Matters

The thickness of your lenses depends on your prescription strength and the frame you select. Higher prescriptions result in thicker edges for minus prescriptions or thicker centers for plus prescriptions. Choosing a higher index material reduces this thickness, creating lenses that are more comfortable, aesthetically pleasing, and compatible with a wider range of frames.

Aesthetics

Thinner lenses look better in your frames, reducing the "bug-eye" effect of strong plus prescriptions or the "small-eye" effect of strong minus prescriptions.

Comfort

Higher index lenses are lighter, making your glasses more comfortable to wear for extended periods without pressure on your nose or ears.

Frame Options

Thinner lenses give you more freedom to choose from a wider variety of frame styles, including delicate rimless and semi-rimless options.

All Your Lens Queries. Answered.

Get answers to common questions about lens options and services.

What lens thickness should I choose for my prescription?

The ideal lens thickness depends on your prescription strength. For mild prescriptions (up to +/-2.00), standard 1.5 index lenses are usually sufficient. For moderate prescriptions (+/-2.00 to +/-4.00), we recommend 1.6 index lenses. Higher prescriptions benefit from 1.67 or 1.74 high-index lenses, which are thinner and lighter.

What's the difference between anti-reflective and blue light coatings?

Anti-reflective coatings reduce all light reflections on the lens surface, improving clarity and reducing glare. Blue light coatings specifically target and filter the high-energy blue light emitted by digital screens and LED lighting. While anti-reflective coatings enhance general vision clarity, blue light coatings are focused on reducing digital eye strain and potential sleep disruption.

Are Transitions® lenses good for driving?

Transitions® lenses do darken while driving, but they may not get as dark as dedicated sunglasses because car windshields block some of the UV light that activates them. For those who spend significant time driving, we recommend either Transitions® XTRActive® lenses, which have some activation behind the windshield, or a separate pair of prescription sunglasses for optimal driving comfort.

How do I know if I need varifocal lenses?

You might benefit from varifocal lenses if you find yourself needing different glasses for different distances, such as removing reading glasses to see far away. Common signs include difficulty reading small print, holding reading material at arm's length, or experiencing eye strain when switching between different viewing distances. An eye examination with our optometrists can determine if varifocals are right for you.

Can I combine multiple coatings?

Yes, you can combine multiple coatings. Popular combinations include anti-reflective + UV protection. Our advisors will help you choose the right combination for your needs, as each coating complements the others to enhance your vision and protection.

What's the difference between polarized and non-polarized tint?

Polarized lenses block reflected light, reducing glare from water, snow, and roads. They're perfect for driving and outdoor activities. Non-polarized lenses only reduce light intensity. Both provide UV protection, but polarized lenses offer better comfort in bright conditions.
